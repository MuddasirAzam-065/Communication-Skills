WHAT IS PROPOSAL WRITING?

A proposal is a formal written document that presents an idea, solution, or plan to persuade a reader ,decision-maker, stakeholder, or client  to approve, fund, or act upon it.

Identify a Problem
Propose a Solution
Persuade & Justify
Clearly states an existing challenge or need that demands attention
Offers a concrete, well reasoned answer to address the problem
Uses evidence, cost benefit analysis and logic to earn approval

### Notes:
SPEAKER NOTES – SLIDE 3 (Student 1 | ~5 min)
Read out the definition. Emphasise the word "persuade" — a proposal is not just an information document, it is a persuasive tool.

Discuss the three pillars:
1. IDENTIFY A PROBLEM – Without a clear problem, there is nothing to propose. The problem must feel real and urgent.
2. PROPOSE A SOLUTION – The heart of every proposal. Must be specific, actionable and realistic.
3. PERSUADE & JUSTIFY – Use facts, data and logic. Avoid emotional appeals without evidence.

Real-life example to share: "Imagine you work in IT at a company. You notice the office internet is too slow and it is hurting productivity. You write a proposal to management to upgrade the network — that is proposal writing in action."

Ask class: "Can anyone give another example of when you might write a proposal at work?" (Wait for 2–3 answers).

KEY MESSAGE: The audience of the proposal completely changes how you write it.
- For internal: You already know the organisation's culture and goals. You use shared language. You don't need to "sell" your organisation — you need to sell your IDEA.
- For external: The reader may know nothing about your company. You must build trust, show credentials, and focus on the CLIENT's needs.

### Notes:
SPEAKER NOTES – SLIDE 7 (Student 2 | ~7 min)
Walk through each step. Use a story-telling approach — frame it like a journey.

Step 1: "Before you write a single word, you must understand the problem deeply. Many proposals fail because they solve the wrong problem."
Step 2: "Your manager cares about different things than your CEO. A department head might care about efficiency. The finance director cares about cost. Always write for YOUR specific reader."
Step 3: "Don't just propose the first solution you think of. Explore 2–3 options and choose the best — and be ready to explain why."
Steps 4–5: "Structure first, words second. An outline stops you going off-topic."
Step 6: "A proposal without evidence is just an opinion. Back every claim with data."
Step 7: "Never submit a first draft. Always re-read — ideally the next morning."
Step 8: "Submit through the right channel. In many organisations you need to present the proposal verbally as well."

Transition: "Now let's look at what GOES inside the proposal — the key sections and elements."

### Notes:
SPEAKER NOTES – SLIDE 8 (Student 3 | ~8 min)
This is the handover to Student 3.

Go through each element. For each one, give 1 sentence of elaboration beyond the slide text.

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY: "Even though it appears first, write it LAST. It summarises the entire proposal. Many executives only read this page — make it count."
PROBLEM STATEMENT: "Be specific. Don't say 'productivity is low.' Say 'Our team processes 40% fewer customer requests per day than the industry benchmark, costing us approximately £12,000 per month in delayed revenue.'"
PROPOSED SOLUTION: "Avoid vague language. Not 'we will improve the system' but 'we will implement X software by Q2, reducing processing time by 50%.'"
BUDGET: "Managers distrust vague budgets. Break everything down. Include hidden costs like training and maintenance."
CONCLUSION / CALL TO ACTION: "End with a clear ask. 'We request approval of this proposal by [date] so that Phase 1 can begin on [date].'"

INTERACTIVE MOMENT: Ask 3 students to each read one element aloud and explain it in their own words.

DOS AND DON'TS IN PROPOSAL WRITING

DO

DON'T

Research the problem thoroughly before writing
Use vague language like 'we will try' or 'maybe'

Use clear, plain language avoid jargon
Exaggerate benefits or underestimate costs

Tailor the proposal to your specific audience
Write for yourself always write for the reader

Support every claim with data or evidence
Submit without proofreading for errors

Keep it concise quality over quantity
Include irrelevant information to bulk up the document

Use headings and visuals for easy scanning
Use a template without customising it

Write the executive summary last
Forget to include a clear call to action

Proofread multiple times before submitting
Send without getting a peer review first

### Notes:
SPEAKER NOTES – SLIDE 10 (Student 4 | ~7 min)
Handover to Student 4.

Emphasise these critical points:
DO — "Data and evidence are non-negotiable. You will not get approval based on gut feeling alone."
DO — "Tailoring is everything. A one-size-fits-all proposal reads as lazy and gets rejected."
DON'T — "Vague language kills proposals. 'We will try to improve productivity' gives management nothing to hold you accountable to."
DON'T — "Never inflate benefits to make the proposal look better. If management approves it and the results don't match, your credibility is damaged."
DON'T — "Forgetting the call to action is like making a great sales pitch and then not asking for the order."

ACTIVITY (2 min): Show one "bad example" sentence and one "good example" sentence. Ask students which is better and why.
Bad: "This new system should improve things and make work easier for everyone."
Good: "Implementing this system is projected to reduce task completion time by 35%, saving approximately 12 staff hours per week."

Ask for a volunteer to "fix" a bad sentence on the spot — makes it memorable and fun.

KEY TAKEAWAYS

A proposal is a persuasive document always write for your reader, not for yourself

Internal proposals are the most common type in professional life master this first

Start with a clear, specific problem vague problems produce vague proposals

Every claim needs evidence data, statistics, and cost-benefit analysis are essential

Follow the structure: Title → Executive Summary → Problem → Solution → Plan → Budget → CTA

The Executive Summary, Budget and Conclusion are the three most-read sections perfect them

Always proofread, get feedback, and tailor your proposal before submission

Q: "What if my manager rejects my proposal?"
A: "Ask for feedback. Find out which section was weak. Revise and resubmit. Most successful proposals go through at least 2 drafts."

A: "There are common structures, but always customise. Your organisation may have its own format — use it."
