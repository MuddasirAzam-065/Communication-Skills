## Slide 3 (Student 1)
Simple words mein, **proposal** ek formal document hota hai jo kisi decision-maker ko convince karta hai ke: *‘Yes, this idea is worth approving or funding.’*

Ab isko aise samajh sakte hain: Proposal has three pillars.

1) **Identify a Problem**: Pehle aap prove karte ho ke problem real hai. Sirf keh dena “issue hai” enough nahi. Aap impact show karte ho—time waste, money loss, quality issues.

2) **Propose a Solution**: Phir aap solution le ke aate ho—clear, actionable. Like *what exactly will change, who will do it, and when*.

3) **Persuade & Justify**: Yahan evidence aata hai—data, cost-benefit, ROI. ROI ka matlab simple: *jo paisa/effort lagay ga, us ke badlay mein organization ko kya faida milega*.

Mini example: Imagine aap university society run kar rahe ho aur attendance low hai. Aap proposal likhte ho for a digital registration + reminders system. Problem: low turnout. Solution: automated reminders. Justification: higher attendance, better event outcomes.

## Slide 5 (Student 2)
Key difference audience ka hai. Aap ne notice kiya hoga internal aur external ka reader change hota hai.

**Internal proposal** mein aap organization ke andar hotay ho. Reader management hoti hai. You share the same environment, so you can be professional but familiar.

**External proposal** mein client hota hai. You need more credibility building—portfolio, capability, stronger sales tone.

Relatable example: Internal is like asking your HOD for new lab equipment—HOD already knows the department needs. External is like pitching your services to a new company—unko pehle trust chahiye.

Transition: *“Theek hai, ab internal business proposals ke common types dekhte hain—so you can imagine real workplace scenarios.”*

## Slide 7 (Student 3)
Hi everyone, I’m Student 3. Proposal writing ko process ki tarah treat karein—warna document messy ho jata hai.

1) **Problem identify**: root cause clear.
2) **Audience know**: manager vs finance—concerns different.
3) **Outline**: headings pehle.
4) **Solution develop**: options compare.
5) **Data add**: numbers, estimates.
6) **Draft write**: simple language.
7) **Review/edit**: grammar + logic.
8) **Submit & follow up**: questions ke liye ready.

Mini story: Ek team ne new software ka proposal diya but costs mention nahi kiye—finance ne reject kar diya. Same idea, but missing data killed it.

Transition: *“Ab process to ho gaya—ab actual proposal ke andar sections kya hotay hain?”*

## Slide 8 (Student 3)
Business proposal ka structure almost aapko roadmap deta hai.

- **Title page**: basics.
- **Executive summary**: snapshot. Aap ne notice kiya hoga executives time-short hotay hain, so this becomes most-read.
- **Problem statement**: clear issue + impact.
- **Proposed solution**: what/how/who/when.
- **Plan/timeline**: milestones.
- **Budget**: breakdown.
- **Benefits & ROI**: measurable gains.
- **Conclusion/CTA**: clear approval request.
- **Appendices**: supporting proof.

Simple words mein: Executive summary = trailer, full proposal = movie.

Transition: *“Ab jab structure samajh aa gaya, let’s talk about best practices—dos and don’ts.”*

## Slide 9 (Student 4)
Assalam-o-Alaikum, I’m Student 4. Dos and don’ts aapki proposal ko accept ya reject karwa sakte hain.

**DO:** research, clear language, tailor to reader, evidence, concise, headings, summary last, proofread.

**DON’T:** vague words like “maybe”, benefits exaggerate, costs hide, template copy, CTA miss.

For example, “We will try to improve productivity” weak sentence hai. Better: “This change will reduce processing time by 30% within 2 months.”

Audience engagement: “Ab yahan ek interesting cheez ye hai—management ko ‘confidence’ chahiye, and confidence data se aata hai.”

Transition: *“Ab last practical part—ek choti si activity karte hain taake aap yeh cheez apply bhi kar sakhein.”*
